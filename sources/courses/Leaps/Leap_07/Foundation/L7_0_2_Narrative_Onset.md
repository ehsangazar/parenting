Your child comes running to you, eyes wide, pointing toward the window and breathlessly uttering a string of words: "Dog... bark... ball... gone!" To the untrained ear, this sounds like a simple list of labels. But for the child, this is a revolutionary moment. They are no longer just naming what they see in the "Eternal Now"; they are attempting to pull a piece of the past into the present. They are trying to tell you a story.

![Three fingers, three moments — the Shared Recounting ritual anchoring the day into permanent personal history](learning/raised-logic/l7-narrative-onset/body-opening-3374fbb1-44e8-4f2c-a54c-587b5de2c016.png)

We are witnessing the neurological birth of **Narrative Logic**. Before this leap, your child's memory functioned like a collection of beautiful, high-resolution snapshots—vivid but disconnected. They lived in a series of isolated moments. But now, the brain is building the first bridges between those snapshots to create a **Mental Timeline**. They are beginning to realize that one event causes another and that "Then" is inextricably linked to "Now." This is the emergence of **Episodic Memory**, the ability to consciously recall and recount personal events in a sequence. When they link "The dog barked" with "The ball is gone," they are constructing their first logical arc of personal history. This isn't just about language; it is the beginning of their ability to make sense of the world's chaos. By weaving events together, they are transforming a confusing stream of sensory data into a coherent and predictable life story.

To support this transition, become their **Temporal Translator**. When they provide you with a list of disjointed objects, offer the "Connective Tissue." If they say "Milk... floor... messy," you respond with, "Ah, the milk spilled on the floor, and now it is messy." By using the language of causality (because, so, then), you are providing the architectural blueprint they need to build their own internal stories.

Practice **Shared Recounting** as a daily ritual. At the end of the day, spend three minutes describing the "Three Big Things" that happened. Use a strict "Before, During, and After" structure. This simple verbal framing acts as a external scaffold for their developing sense of time, helping them anchor their experiences into a permanent, searchable history of their own life.

You are no longer just witnessing a child who talks; you are witnessing the birth of a historian. By helping them connect the dots of their day, you are teaching them that their life is not a series of random accidents, but a meaningful journey that they have the power to name and understand.
