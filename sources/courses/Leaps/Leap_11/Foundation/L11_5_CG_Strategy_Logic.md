You are playing a board game together, and you can see the move they *should* make, the one that will block your next turn and secure their victory. Instead, they lunge for the immediate, shiny prize, only to realize a few minutes later that they've walked right into a trap.

![Leaning over the felt board game — the Mental Chessboard glowing, the Strategic Mind arc forming as the Two-Step Move activates](learning/raised-logic/l11-strategy-logic/body-opening-d97dd93d-e67d-4df1-9d2b-604400eab2cc.png)

Your child is currently constructing the neurological architecture for **Strategic Planning**. This is a significant leap in **Executive Function**, where the brain moves from reactive, "moment-to-moment" thinking to the **Two-Step Move**. The prefrontal cortex is now bridging with the **Working Memory** centers to create a **Mental Chessboard**. This allows the child to not only evaluate the current state of the world but to simulate a *future* state based on a set of consistent rules. To think two steps ahead, the brain must simultaneously hold a hypothetical scenario in mind while suppressing the impulse to grab the immediate reward. It is the birth of the **Strategic Mind**, where they are learning to be both the actor and the director of their own logic.

To help them calibrate their internal simulator, consider these stewardship shifts:

### **Thinking Aloud**
Make your own invisible strategies visible. When you are making a decision, whether it is a move in a game or planning the route to the grocery store, narrate your logic. "If I take this turn, we might hit traffic, but if I wait five minutes, the road will be clear." This **Cognitive Modeling** allows your child to see the "If-Then" architecture of planning in action, providing a blueprint for their own internal simulations.

### **The Strategy Sabbatical**
Shift the focus from "Winning" to "Planning." During games or complex tasks, pause and ask a non-judgmental question: "What do you think my next move will be?" This forces the brain to step out of its own perspective and build a **Mental Model** of another person's logic. By celebrating the accuracy of their prediction rather than the final score, you reinforce the neural habit of looking two steps ahead.

The next time they make an impulsive move, remember they aren't being "careless." They are simply learning to boot up a complex internal simulator that will eventually allow them to navigate the most intricate systems of adult life.
