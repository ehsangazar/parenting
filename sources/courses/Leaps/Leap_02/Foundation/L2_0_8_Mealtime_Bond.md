In the first twelve weeks, your infant's experience of feeding was a deeply internal event: the relief of hunger, the restoration of physical comfort, the warmth of your skin. The external world barely existed during those minutes together. But something has shifted at the table.

![The mouth opens before the spoon arrives — anticipation as a sign of prediction](learning/raised-logic/l2-mealtime-bond/body-opening-063cee03-ea17-4608-b497-40b04a0e07b4.png)

Between four and six months, feeding transforms from a primitive need into a sophisticated exercise in **Mutual Regulation**. Your infant is transitioning into the **Extradyadic Domain**, where they can finally include an object (the bottle, the spoon, or a toy) into the interaction between the two of you. This is the birth of **Joint Attention**, the remarkable cognitive ability to look at an object and then back at you to share the experience. Feeding is no longer just about ingestion; it is about the first shared meanings of your lives together.

This shift is visible in the emergence of **Anticipatory Opening**. You may notice that as the bottle or spoon approaches, your infant opens their mouth in a clear demonstration of **Means-End Reasoning** and cognitive prediction. They are no longer just reacting to the touch of the nipple; they are mentally anticipating the event before it happens. This period also marks the dawn of **Social Referencing**, where they look to your face to gauge your reaction to a new taste or a sudden sound.

**Treat Every Pause as a Turn**
When your infant stops feeding to gaze at you, respond with a smile, a word, or a gentle sound. These pauses are the first vocabulary of joint attention; your infant is practicing the fundamental rhythm of conversational turn-taking long before words arrive.

**Name the Anticipatory Opening**
When you see your infant's mouth open as the bottle or spoon approaches, acknowledge it: "You knew it was coming." This narration reinforces the link between their internal prediction and the external event, building their sense of cognitive competence.

**Follow Their Social Reference**
If your infant looks toward a sudden sound and then back at your face, give them a clear, calm response. This **Social Referencing** is the beginning of their understanding that you are a reliable guide to the meaning of the world around them.

By responding to their pauses and babbles with your own smiles and words, you are teaching them the fundamental rhythm of human connection: the beautiful, lifelong realization that we are nourished by more than just food.
