For the first few months, your infant's visual world was much like a beautiful flat photograph. They could see the patterns of your face and the high-contrast edges of their nursery, but the concept of "far" and "near" was largely a mystery. But now, as if a master technician has finally aligned the lens, a profound optical awakening is taking place.

![The looming response — a blink that proves the brain is calculating depth](learning/raised-logic/l2-3d-world/body-opening-f2d7c75b-3cf1-40f2-992a-3f35d375b8ae.png)

The neurology behind this shift is a feat of **Stereopsis**. Up until now, your infant's brain received two separate images, one from each eye, but struggled to integrate them. Now, the visual cortex is beginning to calculate **Retinal Disparity**, the tiny difference between the left and right eyes' views, to create a single, deep perception of the world. As their eyes achieve better **Convergence**, they can lock onto a single point in space with precision.

This is closely linked to **Size Constancy**, the cognitive realization that your face doesn't actually get smaller as you walk away; it is simply moving through depth. This **Visual-Spatial Calibration** is a prerequisite for every reaching and crawling attempt that will follow. The world is no longer a flat stage; it is a three-dimensional landscape full of surfaces, gaps, and distances to be navigated.

**Create a Landscape of Distance**
Introduce kinetic cues by moving toys slowly toward and away from your infant. This movement through depth gives the visual cortex the data it needs to calculate retinal disparity and build its first three-dimensional map of space.

**Watch for the Looming Response**
Move a rattle slowly toward your infant's nose. A blink or slight pull-back is not a flinch; it is proof that their brain is successfully calculating an impact path, one of the earliest observable signs of true depth perception in action.

**Offer Objects With Fronts and Backs**
Choose toys that have a distinct "front" and "back," then slowly rotate them in space. Tracking an object as it turns teaches the visual cortex to process **Pictorial Depth Cues**, the subtle shadows and textures that specify where one surface ends and another begins.

When your infant looks past you to gaze at a distant window, they aren't losing interest in you. They are marveling at the fact that, for the first time, they can truly see how vast and deep their world really is.
