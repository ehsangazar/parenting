In the first three months, your infant's world was primarily internal. Their movements were centered on their own body, and the environment was a backdrop to their homeostatic journey. But now, something has changed: they want their movements to do something.

![A furrowed brow and a batting hand — the scientist running their first experiment](learning/raised-logic/l2-accidental-agency/body-opening-2258534f-6053-416c-b168-74cb93d6cb4d.png)

This is the shift from **Primary Circular Reactions**, centered on the self, to **Secondary Circular Reactions**, centered on the environment. This stage is defined by the **Accidental Repeat**. Your infant may unintentionally strike a dangling mobile, notice the resulting sound or movement, and then pour immense neural energy into reproducing that effect. This is the dawn of **Contingency Detection**: the first moment they realize that their physical self has the power to change the world around them.

Because your infant is in a state of **Semi-Intentionality**, this period brings a new wave of specific frustrations. They do not yet have a goal in mind from the start; rather, they stumble upon an interesting result and struggle to recreate it. This can lead to a cycle of intense focus followed by sudden outbursts of annoyance when the "repeat" doesn't happen. Their world is becoming a laboratory, and like any scientist, they are finding that the first discovery is often a beautiful, frustrating accident.

**Choose Toys That Answer Back**
Select objects with immediate feedback: toys that rattle when batted, crinkle when squeezed, or swing when kicked. These are not just playthings; they are evidence of your infant's agency, the "yes" that confirms their first scientific hypothesis and fuels the next attempt.

**Set the Stage, Then Step Back**
Place a responsive toy within the arc of their reach, then wait. Resist the urge to guide their hand or demonstrate the action. The discovery must be accidental first; your role is to provide the laboratory, not to run the experiment.

**Respect the Furrowed Brow**
When you see your infant batting a toy with increasing intensity, brow furrowed in concentration, you are witnessing high-stakes **Sensorimotor Integration** in progress. The frustration of a "failed repeat" is as developmentally valuable as the success; it is the essential friction that builds **Means-End Reasoning**.

Your task is to sit back and witness this transition from passenger to first, accidental architect. You are giving them proof of their own agency.
