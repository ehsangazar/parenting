In the quiet of a shared gaze, you might notice how your child waits for your smile before letting out a soft coo, or how they become suddenly unsettled if you look away too long. You are feeling the rhythmic pulse of a baseline expectation that they are born to build.

![The coo rises — the baby watches and waits for the echo](learning/raised-logic/l1-pulse-expectation/body-opening-ea429607-9c3c-4109-9f31-bd2dd6a675fb.png)

By the end of the first three months, your child is no longer just a passive recipient of care; they have become a contingency expert. According to developmental science, infants as young as two months develop a powerful expectation of social responsiveness, often described as the "dance of interaction." They aren't just reacting to you; they are predicting you. Through thousands of minute exchanges, their brain calculates the probability that a specific behavior—like a vocalization or a reaching movement—will elicit a specific response from you. This realization of effectance, the knowledge that they can act upon and alter their environment, is a fundamental engine of cognitive growth.

When this social pulse is rhythmic and predictable, they develop a sense of safety and efficacy. The boundary is clear: your child is constantly scanning for contingency. If the response is delayed or missing, as seen in research like the Still-Face paradigm, their internal world quickly shifts from curious exploration to distress and physiological withdrawal.

**Honor the Contingency Loop**
Prioritize immediate, high-fidelity responses to their non-distress signals, such as coos or intentional gazes. This isn't about constant entertainment; it's about closing the contingency loop. When you respond promptly and appropriately to their bids, you are validating their internal map of cause and effect. This reinforcement builds the neural scaffolding they need to feel effective and secure in their own skin.

**Maintain the Dance Rhythm**
Recognize that your interaction follows a rhythmic pulse of turn-taking. Allow for a natural inhale and exhale in your communication, giving them space to speak with their movements or sounds before you reply. By respecting this timing, you prevent interaction fatigue and ensure that their underpowered processing system can keep up with the social melody. Your consistency is the steady beat that helps them stay regulated.

**Navigate the Still-Face Moments**
Be mindful of the profound impact of your internal state on their sense of reality. When you are distracted or still-faced due to external stress, their system registers a drop in environmental predictability. If you must step away or disengage, do so with a clear, calm transition. By being intentional about your re-entry into the interaction, you help them re-anchor to a baseline of social reliability and repair any temporary breaks in the pulse.

Your responsiveness is the steady rhythm that proves to them they are real, powerful, and seen. You are not just a parent; you are the consistent, heart-beat response that lets them know they have a home in this world.
