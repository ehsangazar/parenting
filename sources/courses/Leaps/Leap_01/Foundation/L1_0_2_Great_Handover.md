The surprise of their grip is undeniable—an iron-like strength that locks onto your finger with a force that feels deeply intentional. You might witness their arms suddenly fly wide as if they are falling through thin air, only to snap back toward their center in a frantic, wide-eyed arc.

![The Moro reflex in full arc — arms wide, a system with no filters](learning/raised-logic/l1-great-handover/body-opening-59abe7e5-8963-4e3b-932d-57834a1449c2.png)

These powerful, all-or-nothing movements are not yet acts of will, but rather the essential software of survival known as reflexes. Your child is currently inhabiting the Great Handover, a biological transition where the primitive brainstem begins the slow process of passing control to the higher cortex. According to developmental science, these involuntary responses—like the Palmar Grasp or the Moro reflex—are the neurological scaffolding required before purposeful agency can emerge. The pyramidal system, which governs precise, skilled movements of the extremities, is still under construction.

To eventually gain the ability to voluntarily reach for a toy or take a step, the brain must first inhibit these subcortical "ghosts." If the reflexive grip stayed forever, they could never learn the nuance of letting go. Their body is effectively a high-performance vehicle being calibrated; the primitive systems are currently in the driver's seat, ensuring safety until the master architect is ready to take the wheel.

**Validate the Primitive Strength**
Honor the integrity of their current neurological status by observing these all-or-nothing sequences without rushing to alter them. When you see the fanning of their toes during a Babinski reflex or the rhythmic search of a rooting reflex, recognize it as evidence of a perfectly functioning brainstem. This isn't just a cute behavior; it is a vital check of their spinal and vestibular systems.

**Provide the Womb-Like Boundary**
Support the transition from reflex to agency by offering consistent physical resistance. When the Moro reflex triggers—that sudden flailing response to sound or loss of support—provide a steady, firm boundary through swaddling or a containment hold. By mimicking the physical limits of the womb, you help dampen the intensity of the reflex, allowing their nervous system to return to a baseline of safety more quickly.

**Simplify the Motor Environment**
Avoid overwhelming their emerging motor systems with excessive tools or positions that demand high-level coordination. Their current boundary is one of gravity and posture, managed by the extrapyramidal system. Design slow-paced interactions where they are fully supported, allowing them to feel the natural weight of their limbs without the pressure of intentional accomplishment.

This reflexive power is the foundation of future freedom. Your child isn't losing their strength; they are trading automatic survival for the majestic, chosen movements of an independent soul. You are witnessing the very first seeds of human intention taking root.
