As the bridge between womb and world is built, you will likely encounter the "weather" of a brain working at full capacity. The **Moro Reflex**—that startled, flailing arc triggered by a door click or a shift in light—can feel like a sign of distress. In reality, these are the symptoms of a system with no filters.

The newborn brain is profoundly sophisticated in recognizing patterns, yet easily overwhelmed by perceptual noise. Every sensation arrives at maximum volume. Your child isn't fragile; they are profoundly awake, mapping the very first boundaries of existence through the sensory feedback of their new home.

![A newborn trembling and wide-eyed in the hospital nursery — a parent close by](learning/raised-logic/l1-welcome/body-opening-7f14a3dc-bdfd-4fa1-8cf8-2e5105eebae0.png)

Your primary objective in this window is to act as the **Curator of Sensory Intake**.

**Design a Low-Arousal Zone**
Rather than introducing toys or complex environments, your task is to design a space that respects their limited neurological energy. Soft lights and consistent, low-decibel sounds provide the scaffolding their brain needs to focus on internal regulation—heart rate, respiration, and temperature.

**Become Their Sensory Anchor**
Your presence is the ultimate landmark—the steady, high-contrast anchor in an otherwise swirling sea of new data. By staying close, consistent, and calm, you give their nervous system a reliable coordinate to return to whenever the world becomes too loud.

**Read the Moro as a Signal**
When the startled arc fires, treat it as information rather than distress. A gentle, firm swaddle or a containment hold responds directly to what their nervous system is asking for: a boundary, a limit, a return to the womb's quiet.

You are not just a caregiver; you are the architect of their first external safe harbor.
