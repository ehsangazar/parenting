A small, determined finger extends toward a bottle on the far counter or a favorite ball that has rolled just out of reach. This gesture is often accompanied not by a general cry of distress, but by a focused "vocal command"—a sharp, urgent sound that specifically demands your engagement. This is not merely a physical reach that fell short; it is the first time your child has employed the world's most sophisticated communicative tool: the human index finger.

![The parent turns to follow the point — the first silent sentence understood](learning/raised-logic/l3-pointing-finger/body-opening-22c11c0f-11b3-4633-8b13-06e22a1d323f.png)

This milestone marks the emergence of **Proto-Imperative Pointing**. Your child has finally realized that you are a separate, capable agent who can be "recruited" to bridge the gap between their desire and their physical reach. This represents a massive leap in **Triadic Interaction**, where for the first time, communication expands to involve three distinct points: the child, the adult, and the target object. Unlike the earlier, reflexive reach, pointing is a deliberate, symbolic act of **Pre-verbal Communication**. They are effectively using you as a "social tool" to manipulate their environment. This behavior is the critical physiological precursor to spoken language, signaling that the brain's **Left Hemisphere** is beginning to organize the world into discrete, nameable "needs." They are no longer just reacting to a feeling of lack; they are dictating a social solution.

**Narration of the Point**
When your child points, provide the "missing labels" immediately and with enthusiasm. Say, "You want the red ball? Here is the ball." This **Contingent Labeling** bridges the gap between their silent intention and the spoken word. By narrating their point, you are reinforcing the **Social-Pragmatic** connection, teaching them that their gesture has the neurological power to elicit a specific, meaningful vocal response from the world. You are validating their first "sentences."

**The Choice Array**
Encourage this precision by offering two objects simultaneously and waiting for the point. By requiring a **Selective Gesture**, you are prompting the brain to inhibit the impulsive, two-handed grab and instead deploy a more precise, communicative act. This strengthens their **Intentional Communication** skills, allowing them to practice the subtle nuance of "this object, and specifically not that one" before they have the complex vocabulary required to say it. You are helping them refine their first orders to the universe.

The first point is the functional end of the era of parental guesswork. Your child is no longer a passive passenger in their own life; they are becoming the director of their experience, using a single finger to command the world to meet them exactly where they are.
