A toddler stands before a closed door, holding a large, awkward plastic truck. They pause, looking at the handle, then at the truck. Instead of the usual frantic banging and pulling that characterized last month's efforts, they simply set the truck down, open the door with both hands, and then pick the truck back up to walk through. That brief, quiet pause is the sound of a revolution.

![Hands hovering, the plan running — the Internal Simulator working before the hands move](learning/raised-logic/l6-mental-representation/body-opening-4b06e693-92cf-431e-a3b5-384e04c46e46.png)

This moment of stillness is the signature of **Mental Representation**, a landmark leap where the brain begins to solve physical problems in the "internal lab" before committing to action. In the previous phase, your child relied on a "trial-and-error" loop, reaching, failing, and adjusting in real-time. Now, the brain has developed the capacity for **Internalized Sensorimotor Actions**. They are creating a mental stand-in for reality, allowing them to "trial" a solution purely in thought. This is the transition from a purely reactive organism to a predictive one. When they pause before an obstacle, they are running a simulation of the outcome, calculating the physics of the door or the height of a shelf within their internal mental map. This shift from physical experimentation to cognitive drafting is the absolute foundation of all future logical reasoning and abstract thought.

**The Strategic Pause**
When you see that "frozen" look of concentration, resist the urge to step in and fix the problem for them. This silence is the brain running its first sophisticated software. By allowing that extra five seconds of processing time, you are giving the **Internal Simulator** the space it needs to boot up and find its own resolution.

**Mirroring the Plan**
Narrate the specific strategy you see them using, rather than just praising the result. Saying "You put the block down first so you could reach the handle" confirms their internal logic and helps them bridge the gap between their mental draft and their physical execution. This verbal mirroring solidifies the **Symbolic Blueprint** they are constructing.

**Tool Logic Challenges**
Introduce tasks that require a two-step mental plan, such as placing a toy inside a clear container that requires a specific orientation to open. Providing these "logic puzzles" encourages the brain to rely more on mental representation than on sheer physical force, strengthening the neural pathways of prediction.

The heavy lifting is no longer happening in their hands; it has moved into their mind. By honoring these moments of stillness as active work, you are witnessing the birth of a thinker who can see the solution before they even touch the problem.
