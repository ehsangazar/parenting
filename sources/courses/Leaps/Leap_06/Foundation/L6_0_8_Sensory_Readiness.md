You notice your child suddenly stop mid-play. Their face goes still, and they stare into space for a heartbeat. Or perhaps they tug at their diaper with a look of mild confusion or retreat into a corner before a bowel movement. This isn’t just a physical event; it is the first time they are truly "tuning in" to an internal broadcast they’ve largely ignored for over a year. This is the dawn of **Sensory Readiness**, where your child begins to map the sensations of their internal world with the same precision they’ve applied to the external one.

![Still in the play, tuning inward — the body's first broadcast, the parent ready to name it gently](learning/raised-logic/l6-sensory-readiness/body-opening-5551986c-f18e-49a0-9cf9-5aacc3011f8c.png)

This leap is powered by **Interoception**—the sensory system that monitors the internal state of the body, including the pressure of a full bladder or bowel. In Leap 6, the **Symbolic Architect** is beginning to apply their new mental mapping skills to their own anatomy. They are learning to connect a vague, internal physical pressure with a specific, external outcome. This is a critical window not for "drills" or "training," but for building a **Toileting Rapport**—a shared language and mutual understanding of these new internal signals. By 24 months, if the child can represent these sensations with a word or a sign, they have reached a major milestone in **Self-Regulation** and **Autonomy**.

To nurture this Claiming-of-the-Self without pressure, you can serve as their "Signal Mirror" through these low-stakes interactions:

**The Sensation Narrative**
When you notice the "stillness" or the "tug," offer a gentle, non-judgmental label for what their body might be feeling. "I see you're standing very still; is your body telling you something? Maybe you're feeling some pressure?" This helps move the internal sensation from the subconscious to the **Representational Mind**. You aren't demanding they use the potty; you are simply helping them "see" their own internal maps, which is the necessary precursor to conscious control.

**The Functional Modeling**
Leverage their love of **Symbolic Play** and **Multistep Sequences**. Use a favorite doll or stuffed animal to act out the bathroom routine—walking to the bathroom, sitting, washing hands. This takes the pressure off "their" body and places the sequence into the world of **Pretense**, where they can master the logic of the routine without the stress of performing. By making the bathroom a natural, low-stakes part of **Domestic Stewardship**, you remove the fear and replace it with a sense of mastery and "helping" the self.

The path to toileting is less a finish line to be crossed and more a conversation to be started—the first great dialogue between your child and their own physical rhythm.
