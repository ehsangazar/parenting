# Raised Survey Analysis (n=31)

**Source:** `SurveyResponse` table, parenting Coolify DB, pulled 2026-05-19
**Window:** 2026-01-30 → 2026-02-17 (~18 days)
**Sample size:** 31 responses, 12 emails captured (39%)
**Status:** Hypothesis-forming. Sample is small and likely convenience-recruited (founder's network), so directional only — not statistically conclusive.

---

## 1. Headline findings

1. **The v1 ICP wedge ("first-time parents, pregnancy → 18 months") does NOT match the people who actually responded.** Only ~13% of respondents have a 0-12 month infant; the **modal age group is 6-12 years (48%)** with strong representation up to teen years. The data is pushing toward a very different wedge (see §6).

2. **Pain is real but moderate, not screaming.** Overwhelm by conflicting advice averages 5.6/10. Trust difficulty averages 4.9/10. Both well above neutral, but not the "8-9/10 doom" that justifies premium SaaS pricing.

3. **They're not daily Googlers.** 39% research development *rarely*, only 13% weekly+ . v1 ICP assumed a "2am search compulsion" persona — that's not what showed up.

4. **Willingness to pay is soft.** Only **19% clear "yes"** (6% definitely + 13% if affordable). 23% clear "no." 58% "maybe, depends on features." Realistic price ceiling is **$5/month** — 74% will pay $0-10, only 6% above $10.

5. **The highest-pain respondents are the most price-sensitive.** The 4 who said "yes, if affordable" had mean overwhelm = 9.0 and mean trust difficulty = 8.0. The 2 "yes, definitely" people had *lower* pain (4.0 / 3.5). **The people who hurt most can't pay full price.** This breaks the "premium positioning to high-pain segment" playbook.

6. **The competitor isn't BabyCenter — it's ChatGPT + Google + friends.** 71% use Google, 68% friends/family, 48% pediatrician. Only ~13% use any dedicated parenting app. **ChatGPT is named by multiple respondents unprompted as their current solution.**

7. **39% use no parenting app at all.** The market gap is bigger than I assumed in v1, but the buying habit isn't established either.

8. **Two strong product validations:**
   - "Grows with the child" matters: 7.9/10 mean importance
   - "Built by parents, for parents" matters: 7.8/10 mean importance

9. **A respondent explicitly flagged the product strategy:** "*Combining evidence-based advice and memories/legacy seem like two disparate ideas.*" Another wrote: "*Such an app should be a trustworthy adviser mainly. Data gathering is a concern also.*" Privacy + focus are surfacing as concerns worth addressing before they become objections at the App Store review level.

## 2. Who actually responded

| Dimension | Distribution |
|---|---|
| **Family structure** | 84% two-parent, 10% single-parent, 6% guardian/other |
| **Number of children** | 39% one, 45% two, 16% three or more |
| **Modal child age** | **6-12 years (48%)**, then 1-3 years (32%), then 13-18 years (32%). Pregnancy only 6%, infants only 13%. Many parents have kids in multiple age bands. |

**Reading this:** the respondent base is not "anxious first-time parents." It's **experienced multi-child parents whose kids are mostly in school**. This is either (a) a sampling bias (founder's network skews to this demographic) or (b) a genuine signal about who the product attracts. Either way, the v1 ICP can't be confirmed by this data.

## 4. The competitor reframe: ChatGPT is the rival

Unprompted, in free-text responses:

- *"By using ChatGPT or even Google search which is equipped with Smart search of Gemini, I don't believe there should be any difficulty!"*
- *"Conflicting advice, now summarized by AI makes it easier but not necessarily more trustworthy."*
- *"Gen-AI is good at that."*
- *"ChatGPT will be overtaken and widely adopted very soon!"*
- One person literally listed *"Ai"* as their current app.

**Implication for positioning:** Raised's pitch cannot just be "we have evidence-based answers." So does ChatGPT (in the user's perception). The wedge has to be **what ChatGPT *can't* do for a parent**:

- Continuity (remembers your child, their age, their history, your values)
- Vetted experts behind the answer, with citations
- Curated structure (an academy + daily tips, not a blank prompt)
- Community of parents going through the same stage
- Privacy and trust (you don't want every ChatGPT log to know your kid's behavioural issues)

## 5. What they actually want

**Top features (% who put it in their top 3):**

| Feature | % |
|---|---|
| Personalized, evidence-based answers | **84%** |
| Expert-reviewed content (pediatricians, child psychologists) | 68% |
| Family calendar & task management | **58%** ← surprising |
| Milestone tracking with developmental insights | 52% |
| Daily tips tailored to my child's age | 48% |
| Community of parents (combined) | ~50% |
| Audio journaling for memories (combined) | ~20% |

**Two surprises:**

- **Family calendar is the #3 most-wanted feature.** This isn't a "parenting advice" need — it's a logistics need. For multi-child households (61% of respondents), calendar/coordination friction is *real*. Worth taking seriously as a feature and as a wedge ("the calendar that knows your family" → upsell into advice/community).

- **Audio journaling: high *interest* score (7.0/10) but low *priority* in top-3 (~20%).** Classic "would be cool, won't actually use" feature. Don't lead marketing with it.

**Top research topics:**

| Topic | % |
|---|---|
| Education and learning activities | 58% |
| Behavior and discipline | 55% |
| Health and safety | 45% |
| Social and emotional development | 42% |
| Feeding / Nutrition | 29% |
| Developmental milestones | 26% |
| Screen time / digital wellness | 16% |
| Sleep | 13% |

Maps directly to school-age + teen pain points. Sleep is low because most respondents are past the baby phase. This is **content strategy ammunition** — write to behaviour, education, social-emotional development first.

## 6. Pricing reality

**Willingness to pay:**

| Response | % |
|---|---|
| Yes, definitely | 6% |
| Yes, if it's affordable | 13% |
| Maybe, depends on features | 58% |
| No, I prefer free apps | 23% |

**Price point they'd accept:**

| Tier | % |
|---|---|
| Free (ad-supported) | 35% |
| $1 - $5/mo | 19% |
| $5 - $10/mo | 39% |
| $10 - $15/mo | 3% |
| $15 - $20/mo | 3% |

**Recommended pricing model:**
- **Sweet spot: $4.99-$7.99/mo** (covers 58% of respondents at their stated tier)
- **Freemium is essentially required.** 35% only want free; another 19% wants <$5. That's 54% of the market gated unless there's a meaningful free tier.
- **No premium tier above $10 is viable** from this sample — 6% would pay it.

**Counter-intuitive insight: don't price-discriminate up to high-pain users.** They're the cheap ones. The "yes, if affordable" cohort has overwhelm=9.0 — they need it, but they're the ones who'll churn at a $10 paywall.

## 8. What changes about the ICP

The v1 ICP was wrong for **this** sample. Either the sample is wrong, or the ICP is. Probably both, partially.

**Most likely truth:**
- The v1 ICP (first-time parents 0-18mo) was an inherited assumption from the parenting-app category, not from Raised's actual data.
- The survey was distributed via the founder's network, skewing to peers (experienced parents of school-age + teen kids).
- The product itself (academy, community, calendar, evidence-based advice, grows-with-child) genuinely *does* serve experienced multi-child parents better than first-time-baby trackers do.

**Proposed v2 wedge** (full doc in `ICP.md`):

> **Experienced, evidence-leaning parents with school-age children (5-12), often with more than one child, who are tired of conflicting Google/ChatGPT answers and want a single trusted source that grows with their family.**

This matches the data more honestly. It also has commercial advantages:
- Less crowded category (the baby-app market is saturated; the school-age space is wide open).
- Multi-kid households = higher LTV ("one subscription, all your kids").
- The 6-12 segment is *adjacent* to the underserved 13-18 teen-parent segment, which is even less competed for.

The 0-18mo segment becomes a **secondary / expansion** wedge, not the primary.

## 10. Caveats

- **n=31.** This is qualitative-strength sampling. Treat everything above as *hypotheses to test*, not facts to act on at scale.
- **Convenience sample.** Skewed by who the founder's network is. Don't make irreversible decisions until a properly recruited n=200 cohort confirms the picture.
- **No geographic data.** Email domains give weak signals (mostly gmail, 2 UK academic/yahoo, 1 US yahoo). Need an explicit country question next round.
- **Question design issues.** `q3_childAges` allowed multi-select with overlapping options ("0-12 months" and "0-12 months (Infant)" both appeared), which makes the age distribution noisy. Fix this in the next survey rev.
