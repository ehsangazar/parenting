# Raised — Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) v2

**App:** Raised
**Domain:** raised.info
**Doc owner:** Marketing
**Status:** v2 (2026-05-20). Revised from v1 after analysing the n=31 survey (`SURVEY_ANALYSIS.md`). See §0 for the version diff.

---

## 0. What changed from v1 → v2

v1 targeted "first-time parents, pregnancy to 18 months." The survey data did **not** support that wedge — only ~13% of respondents had a child under 1, while 48% had a child aged 6-12, and 61% had two or more kids. The v1 ICP was an inherited category assumption, not a Raised-specific one. v2 pivots the wedge to **experienced multi-child parents of school-age kids**, who are underserved and align with what Raised already does well (academy lessons, evidence-based advice, grows-with-child).

The 0-18mo wedge becomes a secondary / expansion segment (see §9).

**Big positioning change:** v1 framed BabyCenter / Wonder Weeks / Huckleberry as the competition. v2 names the real competitor: **ChatGPT + Google + parenting forums.** Survey respondents repeatedly named AI tools unprompted.

---

## 1. One-line ICP

> **Experienced, evidence-leaning parents with school-age children (roughly 5-12), often with more than one child, who are tired of conflicting Google and ChatGPT answers and want one trusted source that fits their family and grows with their kids.**

This is the **wedge**, not the whole market. Raised serves pregnancy → 18+; every marketing channel, ad, and landing page should speak to this slice first. Expansion segments are in §9.

---

## 2. Why this wedge

Anchored in the n=31 survey results (full analysis in `SURVEY_ANALYSIS.md`):

- **Where the actual audience already is.** 48% of respondents had a child aged 6-12; 32% had a teen. Only 13% had an infant. Whether that's pure sampling bias or a real signal, *this is who already engages with Raised content* — meet them where they are.
- **Multi-child households dominate (61% with 2+ kids).** A single subscription serving every kid in the household is a more compelling value prop here than for a baby-only app.
- **Underserved category.** The baby-tracker market is saturated (BabyCenter, Huckleberry, Wonder Weeks). The school-age and tween-parent market is wide open — no clear category leader exists.
- **Pain match.** Top free-text complaints — *"too generic," "doesn't fit my situation," "conflicting advice"* — are loudest in this segment. School-age parenting is where one-size-fits-all advice breaks down hardest.
- **Strong product alignment.** Two validated drivers in the survey:
  - "Grows with the child" = 7.9/10 mean importance
  - "Built by parents, for parents" = 7.8/10 mean importance
  - 84% of respondents want personalised evidence-based answers
  - 68% want expert-reviewed content (pediatricians, psychologists)
  - 58% want a family calendar (logistics is a real pain in multi-kid households)
- **Land-and-expand still works.** This cohort still has kids approaching teen years and many will have younger siblings — the "one app, all ages" pitch lands harder for them than for a brand-new parent.

The trap to avoid: marketing to "all parents." That puts Raised in a head-on collision with BabyCenter on infant SEO terms, where it cannot win.

## 4. Psychographics — who they actually are

**Self-image.** "I've done this before, but every age brings new things I don't know." Pragmatic, not anxious. Less likely to panic-Google and more likely to skim a trusted source while making dinner.

**Information style.** Already uses ChatGPT and Google heavily. Wants better than ChatGPT, not a replacement for it. Distrusts influencer advice ("yellow content of social media," verbatim from a respondent). Wants citations. Has read enough generic parenting articles to know when something is generic.

**Emotional baseline.** Not panicked. *Tired*. Stretched across multiple kids' calendars, schools, behaviours, screen-time battles. The pain isn't "is this normal" — it's *"I don't have time to find the right answer and I'm getting the wrong ones."*

**Top live problems** (from `q21_expertQuestion` free-text — what they wish they could ask an expert right now):
- Screen time / phone / social media for tweens and teens (multiple respondents)
- Behaviour and discipline / managing tantrums
- Adolescent mental health
- Childhood trauma
- Splitting time as a single parent across multiple kids
- Self-worth and friendships at school age
- Preparing a toddler for a new baby
- Sensitive conversations (death, etc.)

**Identity tensions they carry.**
- "I want to use modern, evidence-based parenting" vs "my own parents did it differently and I turned out OK"
- "I want bespoke advice for *my* kid" vs "I don't have time for one more app I have to feed data to"
- "I trust ChatGPT" vs "I know ChatGPT can be confidently wrong"

**Values.** Honesty, expertise, brevity. Will pay $5-7/mo for quality. **Won't pay $15.** Skeptical of data collection (one respondent explicitly flagged "data gathering is a concern"). Hates being marketed to with pastels and "mama tribe" language.

## 5. Jobs-to-be-done (what they hire Raised to do)

Survey-ranked, by top-3 selections in `q14_topFeatures`:

1. **"Give me an answer that fits MY kid, not a generic one."** (84% — personalised evidence-based answers.) *Verbatim: "Very generic." "It's never quite right for my particular situation." "Bespoke solutions — for me and my child."*
2. **"Make sure the answer comes from someone who actually knows what they're talking about."** (68% — expert-reviewed content.) The "ChatGPT problem" — they don't trust the source.
3. **"Help me coordinate my actual household — schools, appointments, multiple kids."** (58% — family calendar/task management.) *Verbatim: "Coordination, planning."* This is the **surprise** finding.
4. **"Tell me whether what my kid is doing is normal — and what to actually do."** (52% — milestone tracking with developmental insights.) Less about checkboxes, more about *"is this a phase or do I need to act?"*
5. **"Show me what to focus on this week without me having to know what to search for."** (48% — daily tips tailored to child's age.)
6. **"Let me talk to parents going through the same stage, without the Facebook-group toxicity."** (~50% combined — community.)

Lower priority (per survey, even though feature exists in product):
- Audio journaling for memories (~20% top-3 — interest is real but it's not why they'd buy).

## 6. Where they live (channels)

**High-leverage organic (start here):**
- Instagram: carousels and reels. School-age + teen-parent voices like Dr. Becky, The Mom Psychologist, Sarah Ockwell-Smith, Phil Boyle, Maggie Dent.
- Newsletters / Substacks: ParentData (Emily Oster), Lisa Damour (teen psychology), Devorah Heitner (Screenwise).
- Podcasts: Dr Becky's *Good Inside*, *Raising Good Humans*, *ParentData*, *Maggie Dent Parental As Anything*. School-age + teen content over-indexes here vs baby content.
- WhatsApp class / school parent groups: hard to market into directly but a parent recommending Raised inside one is the highest-trust acquisition possible. Build a referral loop early.

**High-leverage paid (after positioning is sharp):**
- Meta (IG/FB) lookalike off the 12 captured emails + future signups. Creative: parent voice direct-to-camera describing a real situation (tantrum, screen-time argument, school behaviour note), not stock babies.
- Google search: long-tail behavioural and tween queries ("how to talk to my 10 year old about social media," "is my child's tantrum normal at age 7"). Less competed than baby queries.
- Newsletter sponsorships in the Substacks above — $300-800 a slot, much cheaper than Meta CPM for the same audience.

**Community / partnership:**
- Schools — PTA partnerships, school newsletters. Direct access to the exact age demographic.
- Family therapists, paediatric clinics, school counsellors — referral cards for the "I keep getting asked the same questions" professional.
- NCT (UK) graduates with second kids now school-age — partial overlap with v1 ICP audience.
- Big Brothers/Sisters, single-parent networks for the underserved single-parent secondary segment.

**Do NOT start with:** TV, billboards, Facebook parenting groups (cold branded posts get banned), generic Reddit r/Parenting (toxic), baby-focused channels (wrong audience for this wedge).

## 7. How they talk (use these in copy — verbatim from the survey)

These are exact phrases respondents used. Steal them.

**The frustration with current options:**
- *"Conflicting advice. Time wasted searching and selecting right advice."*
- *"It's never quite right for my particular situation."*
- *"Very generic."*
- *"All sorts of opinions not based on credible/reliable research."*
- *"Google giving you extreme answers — most end up with 'cancer.' Others give you 'consult a doctor' answer that I have known already."*
- *"For food you have one place, for sleep another, for school another, then you need to repeat this so many times based on the age."*
- *"Apps usually lack community, it's a predefined scope of advices, but you are not sure what they are backed up by."*
- *"The junk and yellow content of social media."*
- *"Credible sources that are locally relevant to my context."*

**What they want:**
- *"Bespoke solutions — for me and my child. Not generic."*
- *"Trustworthy adviser."*
- *"Comprehensive all in one place affordable user friendly app."*
- *"Combines evidence-based advice…"* (with the warning: *"…and memories/legacy seem like two disparate ideas"* — beware feature creep in messaging)

**Avoid in copy:** "mama," "mama bear," "warrior," "tribe," "village," "hack," "boss." Any phrasing that sounds like 2018 mommy-influencer. Avoid "for new parents" (this wedge is *not* new). Avoid pregnancy/baby imagery in primary creative.

**Lean into:** *"Evidence-based." "Expert-reviewed." "Bespoke / personalised to your kid." "Built by parents, for parents." "One trusted source." "Beyond what ChatGPT can give you."*

**Visual direction:** see [[raised-design-direction]] (Confident Calm — warm paper + deep teal + amber). Skews more "trusted adviser" than "playful baby brand," which fits this wedge well.

## 8. Anti-ICP (who Raised is NOT for in v2)

Explicitly **de-prioritise** these for the first 90 days. Doesn't mean "never." Means "not now."

- **Brand-new pregnant parents** — saturated category; competing with What to Expect / BabyCenter is expensive. Comes back in §9 as an expansion segment.
- **"I just want a free feeding/sleep tracker" users.** Won't pay; will churn; skew reviews toward "where is feature X." Not the buyer for an adviser product.
- **Crunchy / vaccine-hesitant / alt-medicine-first audiences.** Off-brand for evidence-based positioning.
- **Parents of 18+ kids.** Different needs; small segment in the data.
- **Users who already get all their answers from ChatGPT and are satisfied.** Not worth fighting for in v1 — they're not in market for a paid app.

---

## 9. Expansion segments (in priority order)

Once the school-age wedge is converting:

1. **Parents of teens (13-18).** 32% of survey respondents already have a teen — and the category is even less competed for than school-age. Natural next move; same content engine extended.
2. **Single parents.** 10% of respondents, with distinctively different needs (time, money, multi-kid coordination). High loyalty potential if Raised serves them well. Real free-text quote: *"Ways to split my time as a single parent with two children."*
3. **Co-parent / partner activation.** Pull the other parent into the existing subscription. Adds zero CAC, increases retention.
4. **Pregnant + new parents (the v1 wedge).** Comes later, after the brand is established as "the trusted family app." Easier to fight BabyCenter from a position of strength.
5. **Grandparents / caregivers.** Gift subscriptions; low priority, high margin.
6. **B2B: schools, paediatric clinics, family therapists.** Bulk licensing or referral programmes. Long sales cycle but high LTV.

## 11. Open questions (resolve before v3)

1. **Geography.** UK is the working default — confirm with survey v2's explicit country question.
2. **Is the school-age wedge a sampling artifact or a real signal?** Survey v2 with broader distribution will tell us. If the broader sample re-confirms baby-parent dominance, swing the wedge back. The data should drive this, not gut feel.
3. **App Store launch date** — drives whether marketing leads with "join the waitlist" or "download now."
4. **Free tier shape.** Confirmed needed (54% of respondents won't pay anything or want $1-5). What lives free vs paid? Recommend: free = daily tips, 3 expert answers/month, basic family calendar. Paid ($5.99/mo) = unlimited answers, full academy, community, journaling.
5. **Founder story.** "Built by parents, for parents" was validated as important (7.8/10). Needs a real founder face/voice to land. Who is that publicly? Lin? Or a co-founder positioning?
6. **AI / ChatGPT positioning.** How explicitly does Raised mention ChatGPT in marketing? *"Better than ChatGPT for parents"* is sharp but risks chasing the AI hype cycle. Test both.
7. **Privacy.** What's the actual stance — does any user content train models? This needs an answer before it becomes an objection at launch.
